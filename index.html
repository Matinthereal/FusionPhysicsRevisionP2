Core Revision Topics

Scalar vs. Vector quantities, contact and non-contact forces, gravity, resultant forces, and work done.

Key Concepts
  • Scalars & Vectors: Scalars have magnitude only (speed, distance). Vectors have magnitude and direction (velocity, force).
  • Resultant Forces: The overall force on an object. If zero, the object is at rest or constant velocity (Newton's 1st Law).
  • Work Done: When a force causes an object to move. $W = Fs$.
  • Newton's Second Law: $F=ma$. The acceleration of an object is proportional to the resultant force.

Transverse and longitudinal waves, wave properties, reflection, refraction, sound waves and ultrasound.

Key Concepts
  • Wave Properties: Amplitude, wavelength ($\lambda$), frequency ($f$), and period.
  • Wave Equation: wave speed (v) = frequency (f) × wavelength ($\lambda$).
  • Reflection: Angle of incidence = angle of reflection.
  • Refraction: Change of direction of a wave as it passes from one medium to another.
  • Ultrasound: Sound waves with frequencies higher than the upper audible limit of human hearing. Used in medical and industrial imaging.

Permanent and induced magnets, magnetic fields, the motor effect, and electromagnets.

Key Concepts
  • Magnetic Fields: The region around a magnet where a force acts on another magnet or on a magnetic material.
  • Electromagnetism: A current flowing through a wire produces a magnetic field.
  • The Motor Effect: A force is exerted on a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field.

Detailed life cycle of stars, red-shift, the Big Bang theory, and the role of gravity in celestial orbits.

Key Concepts
  • Stellar Life Cycle: From nebula to main sequence, then branching into red giant/white dwarf path for sun-like stars, or red supergiant/supernova/neutron star/black hole for massive stars.
  • Red-Shift: The wavelength of light from distant galaxies is stretched, providing evidence for the expansion of the universe. The further the galaxy, the greater the red-shift.
  • Big Bang Theory: The universe started from a very small, hot, dense region and has been expanding ever since. Supported by red-shift and Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR).
  • Orbital Motion: Gravity provides the centripetal force that keeps satellites and planets in stable orbits. For a stable orbit, if the speed changes, the radius must also change.

Required Practicals Guide

Method
  1. Set up the apparatus: a clamp stand, a spring with a pointer, and a ruler positioned vertically.
  2. Measure the original (un-stretched) length of the spring.
  3. Hang a known weight (e.g., 100g = 1.0 N) on the spring and measure the new length.
  4. Calculate the extension (new length - original length).
  5. Repeat by adding more weights, recording the force and extension each time.
Analysis

Plot a graph of force (y-axis) against extension (x-axis). It should be a straight line through the origin, showing force is directly proportional to extension (Hooke's Law: $F=ke$). The gradient of the line is the spring constant, $k$.

Variables & Errors
  • Independent Variable: Force applied (N).
  • Dependent Variable: Extension of the spring (m).
  • Control Variable: The spring being used.
  • Potential Error: Parallax error when reading the ruler. Ensure your eye is level with the pointer. Do not stretch the spring beyond its limit of proportionality.
Method
  1. Set up a ripple tank with a shallow layer of water. Use a lamp above to project wave images onto paper below.
  2. Turn on the motor to create waves of a constant frequency.
  3. To measure wavelength ($\lambda$): Place a ruler on the paper and freeze the image (using a strobe light or phone camera) to measure the distance between two consecutive wavefronts.
  4. To measure frequency ($f$): Count the number of waves passing a point in a set time (e.g., 10 seconds) and divide the count by the time.
  5. Calculate wave speed using $v = f\lambda$.
Errors & Safety

It can be difficult to accurately measure the wavelength of moving waves, so freezing the image is crucial for reducing uncertainty. Be careful with water around electrical equipment (the lamp and motor).

Method
  1. Place a Leslie cube on a heat-proof mat.
  2. Fill the cube with boiling water and replace the lid.
  3. Use an infrared detector to measure the intensity of radiation emitted from each of the four surfaces (matt black, shiny black, white, shiny silver).
  4. Ensure the detector is the same distance from each surface for a fair comparison.
Analysis & Conclusion

Compare the readings from the detector. You should find that the matt black surface is the best emitter of infrared radiation, and the shiny silver surface is the worst.

Variables & Safety
  • Independent Variable: The type of surface.
  • Dependent Variable: The intensity of infrared radiation emitted.
  • Control Variables: Distance from the cube, temperature of the water.
  • Safety: Handle boiling water with extreme care to avoid scalding.

Essential Formulas

Weight

$$ W = mg $$

Weight (N) = mass (kg) × gravitational field strength (N/kg)

Work Done

$$ W = Fs $$

Work done (J) = force (N) × distance (m)

Force (Springs)

$$ F = ke $$

Force (N) = spring constant (N/m) × extension (m)

Moment of a force

$$ M = Fd $$

Moment of a force (Nm) = force (N) × distance (m)

Pressure

$$ P = \frac{F}{A} $$

Pressure (Pa) = force normal to a surface (N) / area of that surface (m²)

Distance

$$ s = vt $$

Distance (m) = speed (m/s) × time (s)

Acceleration

$$ a = \frac{\Delta v}{t} $$

Acceleration (m/s²) = change in velocity (m/s) / time taken (s)

Resultant Force

$$ F = ma $$

Resultant Force (N) = mass (kg) × acceleration (m/s²)

Kinetic Energy

$$ E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 $$

Kinetic Energy (J) = 0.5 × mass (kg) × speed² (m/s)²

Gravitational Potential Energy

$$ E_p = mgh $$

Gravitational Potential Energy (J) = mass (kg) × gravitational field strength (N/kg) × height (m)

Power (Energy Transferred)

$$ P = \frac{E}{t} $$

Power (W) = energy transferred (J) / time (s)

Power (Work Done)

$$ P = \frac{W}{t} $$

Power (W) = work done (J) / time (s)

Efficiency

$$ Efficiency = \frac{\text{useful energy transferred}}{\text{total energy input}} $$

Efficiency = useful energy output / total energy input (often × 100% for percentage)

Wave Speed

$$ v = f\lambda $$

Wave speed (m/s) = frequency (Hz) × wavelength (m)

Period of a Wave

$$ T = \frac{1}{f} $$

Period (s) = 1 / frequency (Hz)

Magnification (Lenses)

$$ M = \frac{\text{image height}}{\text{object height}} $$

Magnification = image height / object height

Force on a Conductor

$$ F = BIL $$

Force (N) = magnetic flux density (T) × current (A) × length (m)

Transformer Equation

$$ \frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{n_p}{n_s} $$

Primary PD / Secondary PD = Primary turns / Secondary turns

Transformer Power

$$ V_p I_p = V_s I_s $$

Primary PD × Primary Current = Secondary PD × Secondary Current (for 100% efficiency)

Predicted Content & Key Areas for 2025

Based on recent exam trends and the PMT Predicted Paper 2025, here are some high-probability topics, question types, and practical considerations for both Combined and Triple Science students.

Forces and Motion
  • Calculations: Expect questions involving $F=ma$, momentum ($p=mv$), work done ($W=Fs$), elastic potential energy ($E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$), and kinetic energy ($E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$). Be prepared for multi-step calculations.
  • Stopping Distance: Detailed explanations of thinking distance and braking distance, and factors affecting them (e.g., driver's reaction time, road conditions, vehicle condition).
  • Collisions & Safety: Conservation of momentum in closed systems. How safety features like airbags and seatbelts reduce injury by increasing impact time (and thus reducing force).
  • Moments and Levers: Understanding and calculating moments. Applying the principle of moments for equilibrium.
  • Pressure in Fluids: Calculations involving pressure, force, and area, including pressure in liquids ($P = h\rho g$). Explaining concepts like terminal velocity.
Waves
  • Wave Properties & Equation: Firm understanding of transverse vs. longitudinal waves, amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed. Expect calculations using $v=f\lambda$.
  • Reflection and Refraction: Ray diagrams for lenses (convex and concave) and mirrors. Explanations of how light bends when entering different media, including changes in speed and wavelength.
  • Electromagnetic Spectrum: Properties and uses of different EM waves (e.g., radio waves for communication, X-rays for imaging). Comparisons between EM waves and sound waves.
  • Infrared Radiation: Emission and absorption by different surfaces (e.g., Leslie Cube findings - matt black is best emitter/absorber).
Electricity and Magnetism
  • Motor Effect: Explanation of how a force is exerted on a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field, and how this causes rotation in a motor. Fleming's Left-Hand Rule is key.
  • Electromagnets: Factors affecting the strength of an electromagnet (current, number of turns, core material). Applications like electric bells or circuit breakers.
  • Transformers & Generators: How transformers change voltage (step-up/step-down) and the role of the iron core. Explanation of how alternators and dynamos induce current (electromagnetic induction) and the function of slip rings/commutators.

Detailed knowledge of the methods, variables, and potential errors for these practicals is essential, as exam questions often involve analyzing or describing them.

  • Investigating the Extension of a Spring: Understanding Hooke's Law ($F=ke$), plotting force-extension graphs, and identifying the limit of proportionality. (See 'Force & Extension' practical in Practicals section).
  • Investigating Waves in a Ripple Tank: Measuring wavelength and frequency to calculate wave speed. Observing reflection and refraction phenomena. (See 'Waves in a Ripple Tank' practical in Practicals section).
  • Investigating Infrared Emission and Absorption: Using a Leslie Cube to show that matt black surfaces are the best emitters/absorbers. (See 'Infrared Emission' practical in Practicals section).
  • Investigating Refraction of Light: Using a ray box and glass block to determine how the angle of refraction varies with the angle of incidence. Drawing ray diagrams accurately.
  • Investigating the Strength of an Electromagnet: Factors affecting strength (number of turns, current, core material).
  • Life Cycle of Stars: Detailed comparison of the life cycles of stars with similar mass to the Sun (red giant, white dwarf, planetary nebula) vs. stars much more massive than the Sun (red supergiant, supernova, neutron star, black hole). Understanding the role of nuclear fusion and formation of elements.
  • Red-shift & Big Bang Theory: In-depth explanation of red-shift as evidence for an expanding universe. How this supports the Big Bang theory. Understanding Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR) as further evidence.
  • Orbital Motion: Gravitational force as the centripetal force for satellites and planets. Relationship between orbital speed and radius for stable orbits.
